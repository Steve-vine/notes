*ISE Canon — the core concepts of ISE and how it works. A living reference; concepts are added as they are agreed.*

## The two layers: Signals and Incidents

Everything ISE reacts to falls into one of two layers, and keeping them separate is the central idea.

- **Signals** are *transient* and *machine-owned*. They reflect the current state of a system and come and go on their own as that state changes. There are two kinds — **Alerts** and **Observations**. Nobody has to "deal with" a signal; it is simply present or not.
- An **Incident** is *durable* and *human-owned*. It is the unit of work. Once opened it must be managed through to resolution by a person — it does not disappear just because the signals underneath it did.

The point of the Incident is to **disambiguate the transience of signals**. Alerts and Observations may flap; the Incident holds steady so a human has one stable thing to work, with a full history of what happened underneath it.

A guiding principle sits behind the two kinds of signal: **ISE defers detection to a source when that source has its own detection layer, and detects for itself only where the source does not.** DataDog has monitors, so ISE trusts them (Alerts). Kubernetes has no equivalent, so ISE forms its own judgement there (Observations).

## Record types

### Alert
A pre-defined failure state detected by an integration's *own* monitoring, which has decided a threshold or condition was breached. ISE forwards it rather than re-deriving it. Alerts are created in ISE with the details the integration provides.

- **Intake:** fast / event-driven — read from the integration as its state is polled, so an Alert surfaces as quickly as possible.
- **Identity & dedup:** keyed on the source's **stable identity plus trigger group** — for DataDog, `monitor/{id}/{group}`, never the display name (names collide and can be edited). A *multi-alert* monitor (one definition grouped e.g. `by host`) produces **one Alert per triggering group**: "Agent not responding on Server X" and "…on Server Y" are two distinct Alerts. The group key doubles as the affected-infrastructure entity for the estate graph. A history of occurrences (last triggered, recurrences) is kept on the record.
- **Carries** the source's severity, mapped onto the canonical ladder (see *Severity, confidence & incident thresholds*).
- **Status:**
  - **Triggered** — currently firing.
  - **Recurring** — has triggered before within its lifetime window (lifetime configurable in settings).
  - **Recovered** — stopped firing on its own.
  - **Ignored** — a known false/unwanted alert; occurrences are still recorded but it stays suppressed and never opens or reactivates an Incident.
  - **Resolved** — *derived*, never set directly (see *Signal resolution & suppression*).

### Observation
An anomaly or issue ISE itself detects while checking an integration that has no detection layer of its own. This is ISE's *opinion*, so it is inherently lower-precision than an Alert and carries an ISE **confidence** ("how sure") alongside its severity ("how bad").

- **Intake:** the **Obs Loop** (below), on a slow schedule — and additionally gathered on demand while investigating an Incident.
- **Dedup:** by event *and* affected infrastructure (the estate entity); a record per observation keeps a history of when it was seen.
- **Carries** an ISE-assigned canonical severity and a confidence (see *Severity, confidence & incident thresholds*).
- **Status:**
  - **New** — detected for the first time.
  - **Recurring** — seen before within its lifetime window (configurable in settings).
  - **Recovered** — went away on its own / not seen again.
  - **Ignored** — known and unwanted in the queue; still recorded, never opens or reactivates an Incident.
  - **Resolved** — *derived*, never set directly (see below).

### Signal resolution & suppression
A signal's **Resolved is never set directly** — it is *derived* by cascade when its Incident is resolved, so "fixed" has a single source of truth.

- **Normal path:** signals go green (Recovered) → a human Resolves the Incident → the signals read Resolved.
- **Early-resolve path:** a human Resolves an Incident while its signals are *still firing* → those signals are forced to Resolved too.
- **Re-assertion:** if a Resolved signal is reported again, that is a fresh trigger → it **Reactivates** the Incident and the signal returns to Triggered/Recurring.
- **Escape hatch:** to stop a persistent/known signal from reopening, set the *signal* to **Ignored** — the durable suppression.

Consequence: a *continuously* firing signal will bounce straight back after an early-resolve (it re-asserts on the next poll), so early-resolve only "sticks" against a persistent signal if it is also Ignored. This is intended. The only two direct human actions on a signal are **Ignore** and adjusting its severity (see below); everything else about a signal is machine-driven.

### Incident
A unique, durable investigation of related Alerts and/or Observations. The Incident is the timeline of what happened, who did what, and when.

- **Opened by:** an Alert (automatically, above the threshold); a *severe, high-confidence* Observation (automatically, above the threshold); or a person, manually, from any signal.
- **Correlation (ingest):** one signal → at most one *open* Incident. A firing Alert either opens a new Incident or attaches to the existing open one for that signal; recovered-and-re-triggered signals re-attach rather than spawning duplicates.
- **Correlation (investigation):** related signals across the estate are added during the targeted investigation. Where several Incidents turn out to share a root cause, **ISE proposes a merge and a human accepts or rejects it** — merging is never automatic, because it is a judgement that needs the estate graph.
- **Resolution is human-owned:** a signal clearing is a *hint* ("Recovered"), never an automatic close.
- **Status:**
  - **New** — newly created.
  - **Active** — assigned to a person to deal with.
  - **Resolved** — believed fixed; may reactivate if it recurs.
  - **Reactivated** — was Resolved but detected again within the reactivation window.
  - **Closed** — Resolved for more than *x* days without recurring; moves here automatically (*x* configurable). A recurrence after Closed is a *new* Incident. (The correlation key must persist on a Resolved-but-not-Closed Incident so a recurrence can reattach.)

## Severity, confidence & incident thresholds

Every signal is placed on one **canonical severity ladder**, and the decision to auto-open an Incident is made once against it — so the rung means the same thing everywhere.

The ladder: **info < low < medium < high < critical**.

- **Alerts** get their severity from a per-connector mapping of the source's native levels (DataDog: `Alert→high, Warn→medium, No Data→low`). A source's scale is a subset of the ladder — DataDog cannot natively produce `critical`, so a critical Incident can only come from an Observation or another integration. That is the point of normalising: the ladder is a superset of any one source's scale.
- **Observations** are *born* with an ISE-assigned canonical severity ("how bad") plus a **confidence** ("how sure") — two distinct axes.

**Auto-incident policy is global, tuning is local.**
- A single **global auto-incident threshold** on the ladder (e.g. ≥ High) expresses policy: "we act on High and above," true everywhere.
- Sensitivity is tuned not by moving the threshold per integration, but by a **severity-override layer** over the connector defaults: `effective severity = override ?? connector default`. Overrides are **scoped** (per integration, down to signal-type or entity) and **auditable**, and never mutate the connector default (a connector upgrade must not wipe local tuning; history shows "downgraded high→medium by <person>"). *Example: pod restarts in the g5 cluster are semi-normal, so that observation type is overridden `high→medium`; with the threshold at ≥ High it no longer auto-opens Incidents, but is still recorded and available as evidence.*

**The open rule:**
- **Alerts** (high-confidence by construction): auto-open if effective severity ≥ threshold.
- **Observations**: auto-open if effective severity ≥ threshold **and** confidence ≥ a global confidence bar; otherwise they remain Observations for a human to promote.
- The threshold governs **automation only** — a human can always open an Incident manually from any signal below the bar.

**Two tools, one gradient:** *downgrade severity* = "keep showing me, keep it as evidence, just don't open an Incident for each one"; *Ignore* = "don't show me at all." Downgrade is the soft, common tuning; Ignore is the hard mute. For this to work in practice, downgrading must be a one-click action *from where the noise appears* (the Incident/Observation in the queue) — tuning as a byproduct of use, like maintaining the estate graph.

## The Obs Loop

The recurring Observation check. It runs on a regular, deliberately *slow* cadence — expensive detection work belongs on a loop measured in hours/daily, not minutes. Configurable in settings: each integration can be opted in or out, and its schedule set independently.

Each pass checks an integration for unusual or anomalous behaviour, records Observations, and captures what it sees as a **baseline of record** — the "normal" that later investigations compare against.

## The investigation flow

The heart of "how it works", triggered by an Alert (or a severe/human-raised Observation):

1. ISE opens an **Incident**.
2. It gathers evidence from the *reporting* integration — for a DataDog alert, the related logs and metrics around the event.
3. It **pivots across the estate** to related systems — the Kubernetes cluster the affected service runs on, the database it depends on — using the estate graph.
4. It cross-references the evidence to form a hypothesis of cause.
5. It **recommends** a course of action (the propose → approve → execute remediation loop).
6. A person reviews the Incident and either approves the recommended remediation or refines it in conversation with ISE.

Expensive AI work therefore happens **per Incident, on confirmed signal, at human pace** — never on an idle timer.

## Applying the model: DataDog (worked example)

DataDog already runs the "is something wrong?" evaluation continuously, over data ISE never ingests. So:

- **Monitors → Alerts.** Only monitor state produces Alerts, keyed by `monitor/{id}/{group}` so multi-alert monitors split correctly per scope.
- **Metrics, events, logs, dashboards, service map → not issue sources.** They are **evidence, gathered on demand** during an Incident investigation to explain *why* a monitor tripped.

This avoids duplicating detection DataDog has already done and avoids surfacing signals DataDog itself doesn't consider alert-worthy. Contrast Kubernetes: no monitor layer, so ISE detects there itself → Observations.

## The Estate Graph

A model of the infrastructure and application estate: **entities** (services, clusters, databases, hosts) and their **relationships** ("Service-X runs on Cluster-Y and depends on Database-Z"). It is distinct from a `System` (a connected integration instance, e.g. the DataDog account or the g5 cluster) — estate entities live *across and inside* Systems.

Why it matters:
- It is the **join key** that lets an investigation pivot from one integration to another (DataDog's `service:service-x` ↔ the K8s deployment `service-x`). Its core job is **identity resolution** — a canonical entity plus a mapping to how each integration names it.
- It **scopes an alert's blast radius** and powers Incident **merging**.
- It makes investigations **cheaper and sharper** — the agent traverses to the right systems instead of exploring blindly.

Maintenance: **discovered where possible, authored where not.** Entities and candidate links are discovered from the integrations (DataDog service map, K8s labels/namespaces); a human confirms them and adds the cross-system semantics no single integration knows. The burden is kept down by making maintenance a *byproduct of use* — when an investigation hits a gap ("where does Service-X's database live?"), ISE asks, and the answer enriches the graph. It can start thin: entities + which integrations they map to + coarse dependency edges is enough to begin.

## Why it's shaped this way

Detection already exists inside the sources, so ISE forwards Alerts cheaply and deterministically. ISE spends real compute only where it must — on Observations, on a slow loop — and only on confirmed signal, inside an Incident investigation a human cares about. The design keeps the expensive, AI-driven work off idle timers and pointed at things that are actually happening.
