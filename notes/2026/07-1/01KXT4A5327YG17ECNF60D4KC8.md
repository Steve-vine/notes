*ISE Canon — the core concepts of ISE and how it works. A living reference; concepts are added as they are agreed.*

## The two layers: Signals and Incidents

Everything ISE reacts to falls into one of two layers, and keeping them separate is the central idea.

- **Signals** are *transient* and *machine-owned*. They reflect the current state of a system and come and go on their own as that state changes. There are two kinds — **Alerts** and **Observations**. Nobody has to "deal with" a signal; it is simply present or not.
- An **Incident** is *durable* and *human-owned*. It is the unit of work. Once opened it must be managed through to resolution by a person — it does not disappear just because the signals underneath it did.

The point of the Incident is to **disambiguate the transience of signals**. Alerts and Observations may flap; the Incident holds steady so a human has one stable thing to work, with a full history of what happened underneath it.

A guiding principle sits behind the two kinds of signal: **ISE defers detection to a source when that source has its own detection layer, and detects for itself only where the source does not.** DataDog has monitors, so ISE trusts them (Alerts). Kubernetes has no equivalent, so ISE forms its own judgement there (Observations).

## Record types

### Alert
A pre-defined failure state detected by an integration's *own* monitoring, which has decided a threshold or condition was breached. ISE forwards it rather than re-deriving it. Alerts are created in ISE with the details the integration provides.

- **Intake:** fast / event-driven — read from the integration as its state is polled, so an Alert surfaces as quickly as possible.
- **Identity & dedup:** keyed on the source's **stable identity plus trigger group** — for DataDog, `monitor/{id}/{group}`, never the display name (names collide and can be edited). A *multi-alert* monitor (one definition grouped e.g. `by host`) produces **one Alert per triggering group**: "Agent not responding on Server X" and "…on Server Y" are two distinct Alerts. The group key doubles as the affected-infrastructure entity for the estate knowledge base. A history of occurrences (last triggered, recurrences) is kept on the record.
- **Carries** the source's severity, mapped onto the canonical ladder (see *Severity, confidence & incident thresholds*).
- **Status:**
  - **Triggered** — currently firing.
  - **Recurring** — has triggered before within its lifetime window (lifetime configurable in settings).
  - **Recovered** — stopped firing on its own.
  - **Ignored** — a known false/unwanted alert; occurrences are still recorded but it stays suppressed and never opens or reactivates an Incident.
  - **Resolved** — *derived*, never set directly (see *Signal resolution & suppression*).

### Observation
An anomaly or issue ISE itself detects while checking an integration that has no detection layer of its own. This is ISE's *opinion*, so it is inherently lower-precision than an Alert and carries an ISE **confidence** ("how sure") alongside its severity ("how bad").

- **Intake:** the **Obs Loop** (below), on a slow schedule — and additionally gathered on demand while investigating an Incident.
- **Dedup:** by event *and* affected infrastructure (the estate entity); a record per observation keeps a history of when it was seen.
- **Carries** an ISE-assigned canonical severity and a confidence (see *Severity, confidence & incident thresholds*).
- **Status:**
  - **New** — detected for the first time.
  - **Recurring** — seen before within its lifetime window (configurable in settings).
  - **Recovered** — went away on its own / not seen again.
  - **Ignored** — known and unwanted in the queue; still recorded, never opens or reactivates an Incident.
  - **Resolved** — *derived*, never set directly (see below).

### Signal resolution & suppression
A signal's **Resolved is never set directly** — it is *derived* by cascade when its Incident is resolved, so "fixed" has a single source of truth.

- **Normal path:** signals go green (Recovered) → a human Resolves the Incident → the signals read Resolved.
- **Early-resolve path:** a human Resolves an Incident while its signals are *still firing* → those signals are forced to Resolved too.
- **Re-assertion:** if a Resolved signal is reported again, that is a fresh trigger → it **Reactivates** the Incident and the signal returns to Triggered/Recurring.
- **Escape hatch:** to stop a persistent/known signal from reopening, set the *signal* to **Ignored** — the durable suppression.

Consequence: a *continuously* firing signal will bounce straight back after an early-resolve (it re-asserts on the next poll), so early-resolve only "sticks" against a persistent signal if it is also Ignored. This is intended. The only two direct human actions on a signal are **Ignore** and adjusting its severity (see below); everything else about a signal is machine-driven.

### Incident
A unique, durable investigation of related Alerts and/or Observations. The Incident is the timeline of what happened, who did what, and when.

- **Opened by:** an Alert (automatically, above the threshold); a *severe, high-confidence* Observation (automatically, above the threshold); or a person, manually, from any signal.
- **Correlation (ingest):** one signal → at most one *open* Incident. A firing Alert either opens a new Incident or attaches to the existing open one for that signal; recovered-and-re-triggered signals re-attach rather than spawning duplicates.
- **Correlation (investigation):** related signals across the estate are added during the targeted investigation. Where several Incidents turn out to share a root cause, **ISE proposes a merge and a human accepts or rejects it** — merging is never automatic, because it is a judgement that needs the estate knowledge base.
- **Resolution is human-owned:** a signal clearing is a *hint* ("Recovered"), never an automatic close.
- **Status:**
  - **New** — newly created.
  - **Active** — assigned to a person to deal with.
  - **Resolved** — believed fixed; may reactivate if it recurs.
  - **Reactivated** — was Resolved but detected again within the reactivation window.
  - **Closed** — Resolved for more than *x* days without recurring; moves here automatically (*x* configurable). A recurrence after Closed is a *new* Incident. (The correlation key must persist on a Resolved-but-not-Closed Incident so a recurrence can reattach.)

## Severity, confidence & incident thresholds

Every signal is placed on one **canonical severity ladder**, and the decision to auto-open an Incident is made once against it — so the rung means the same thing everywhere.

The ladder: **info < low < medium < high < critical**.

- **Alerts** get their severity from a per-connector mapping of the source's native levels (DataDog: `Alert→high, Warn→medium, No Data→low`). A source's scale is a subset of the ladder — DataDog cannot natively produce `critical`, so a critical Incident can only come from an Observation or another integration. That is the point of normalising: the ladder is a superset of any one source's scale.
- **Observations** are *born* with an ISE-assigned canonical severity ("how bad") plus a **confidence** ("how sure") — two distinct axes.

**Auto-incident policy is global, tuning is local.**
- A single **global auto-incident threshold** on the ladder (e.g. ≥ High) expresses policy: "we act on High and above," true everywhere.
- Sensitivity is tuned not by moving the threshold per integration, but by a **severity-override layer** over the connector defaults: `effective severity = override ?? connector default`. Overrides are **scoped** (per integration, down to signal-type or entity) and **auditable**, and never mutate the connector default (a connector upgrade must not wipe local tuning; history shows "downgraded high→medium by <person>"). *Example: pod restarts in the g5 cluster are semi-normal, so that observation type is overridden `high→medium`; with the threshold at ≥ High it no longer auto-opens Incidents, but is still recorded and available as evidence.* These overrides are the structured *tuning* register of the estate knowledge base (below).

**The open rule:**
- **Alerts** (high-confidence by construction): auto-open if effective severity ≥ threshold.
- **Observations**: auto-open if effective severity ≥ threshold **and** confidence ≥ a global confidence bar; otherwise they remain Observations for a human to promote.
- The threshold governs **automation only** — a human can always open an Incident manually from any signal below the bar.

**Two tools, one gradient:** *downgrade severity* = "keep showing me, keep it as evidence, just don't open an Incident for each one"; *Ignore* = "don't show me at all." Downgrade is the soft, common tuning; Ignore is the hard mute. For this to work in practice, downgrading must be a one-click action *from where the noise appears* (the Incident/Observation in the queue) — tuning as a byproduct of use, like maintaining the estate knowledge base.

## The Obs Loop

The recurring Observation check. It runs on a regular, deliberately *slow* cadence — expensive detection work belongs on a loop measured in hours/daily, not minutes. Configurable in settings: each integration can be opted in or out, and its schedule set independently.

Each pass checks an integration for unusual or anomalous behaviour, records Observations, and captures what it sees as a **baseline of record** — the "normal" that later investigations compare against. It also validates the estate knowledge base against reality, surfacing drift as low-severity Observations. It is mostly *cheap deterministic detectors* (known failure modes) plus context-driven suppression, with AI reserved for genuine novelty — not an expensive model scanning everything, which is the pattern that drains budget.

## The Incident Loop

Once an Incident is open, it is worked through a single loop that both *uses* what ISE has learned and *adds* to it. This closed feedback loop is what makes ISE cheaper and sharper with use; it extends the propose → approve → execute remediation loop with a learning bookend at each end.

1. **Raised** — from an Alert, a severe/high-confidence Observation, or a person.
2. **Recall** — cheap retrieval: match the Incident against **memory** (past incident instances with a similar signature/entity — "this looks like last month's connection-pool exhaustion on Kora") and **playbooks** (distilled procedures for this class of problem). Reframes the work from "solve from scratch" to "verify a prior."
3. **Investigate the real state** *(collaborative)* — gather current evidence, directed by knowledge-base traversal and any matched playbook, and test the recalled hypotheses against reality. A prior is a hypothesis to verify, never an answer to run.
4. **Propose resolution** *(collaborative)* — recommend a remediation via propose → approve → execute; default-deny, human-approved (ADR 0017/0021), refinable in conversation (ADR 0024).
5. **Validate resolution** *(collaborative)* — confirm the fix worked. This is the *same mechanism* as the Incident status model: validation confirms enough to **Resolve**; the reactivation window catches regressions; Closed-after-*x*-days-quiet is final validation.
6. **Update the knowledge base** — write back what was learned: the incident instance (automatic history, including *failed* attempts, which are as valuable as successes), any newly-discovered structure/context, and a **playbook** distillation (AI-proposed, human-confirmed — a bad playbook is confidently-wrong advice feeding remediation). Feeds the next Incident's Recall.

**Self-tiering.** Cost and human effort scale with novelty, automatically: a strong memory/playbook match short-circuits — quick confirm, high-confidence proposal, a rubber-stamp approval — while a novel Incident goes deep and heavily collaborative. Compute is spent in proportion to how new the problem is, and **cost per Incident falls as memory grows** — the inverse of a cold-start-every-time system.

**Two failure modes designed against:** *over-fitting to memory* (a new cause wearing a familiar signature) — priors must be rejectable by the investigation, which is explicitly prompted to consider "what if this isn't the usual cause?"; and *human fatigue* — mitigated by the self-tiering above, asking for proportionate involvement.

The three collaborative steps run over the existing chat surface (ADR 0022/0024). What is genuinely new versus today's diagnose → propose → execute → verify is the two bookends — **Recall** at the front and **Update** at the back — which wrap the existing remediation loop in a learning layer rather than replacing it.

### Incident signatures — how Recall matches
Recall turns on a signature, and there are *two*, computed at different moments for different jobs:

- **Trigger signature** — computed at Recall, cheaply, from the signal identity (`monitor/{id}/{group}`, or observation type) + the affected entity/entity-type. Coarse; its job is *retrieval*.
- **Diagnostic signature** — computed after Investigate, from the *cause found* (the failure mode). Precise; its job is *learning* — memory and playbooks are keyed on it.

The split is load-bearing because trigger↔cause is **many-to-many**: one trigger ("agent not responding") can have several causes; one cause (a DB outage) trips many triggers. So Recall matches on the trigger to get into the neighbourhood, and the diagnostic signatures of the priors it retrieves say which causes to check first.

- **Build signatures from stable attributes** (signal type, entity identity, failure mode), never volatile ones (event ids, timestamps, exact counts, time-varying title text). This is the `findings_fingerprint` lesson (ISE-44) — that fingerprint deliberately excludes `last_seen_at`; a volatile signature either never matches (no learning) or matches on noise (false learning).
- **Graded, structured-first matching.** Ranked similarity with a confidence, not an equality test: deterministic structured filters (same signal type + entity type + topology shape) narrow the set, semantic similarity ranks within it. Structured-first keeps Recall cheap and — because a human validates the recall — explainable.
- **Hung off the entity graph.** The backbone is `(signal-type, entity)`, so memory attaches to entities in the knowledge base (Kora carries its own incident history) and Recall pulls priors off the same graph it traverses for evidence — no separate index to keep in sync.
- **Layered by specificity and recency.** Match exact (this signal on this entity) → entity-general (this signal type on this entity type) → class (this failure mode anywhere), weighted by specificity and recency (the estate changes; an old match is a weak hint). Playbooks key on the coarse (class) level; memory on the fine (instance) level.
- **Loose but verified.** The dial runs from loose-match/false-recall to tight-match/no-learning; resolve it by biasing to *loose recall for consideration* and letting Investigate reject. Generous matching is safe because recall never acts — only the human-approved proposal does.

### Playbooks
A playbook is the distilled, reusable form of "how we handle this class of problem" — a *promoted, human-blessed generalisation* over incident memory (which is raw instances, captured automatically).

**Anatomy** (six parts):
1. **Applicability** — the class-level signature (+ optional context conditions) that decides when it fires; what Recall matches against.
2. **Ranked hypotheses** — likely causes and how to tell them apart; turns a coarse trigger match into a directed investigation.
3. **Investigation plan** — the ordered evidence to gather, as *intents* not literal calls. This field *is* the scoped evidence plan that replaces the blind tool loop.
4. **Remediation options** — candidate fixes, each tied to a cause and each a **reference to a governed action** from the connector's `action_catalogue()` (`ActionSpec`, with tier and parameters).
5. **Validation criteria** — how to confirm success; feeds the loop's Validate step.
6. **Efficacy & provenance** — authored-by / derived-from, a live success/failure record, last-used, confidence.

**Structured where it touches governance, narrative where it touches reasoning.** Structured: applicability, action references, validation (matching and governance must be machine-readable). Narrative: hypotheses and evidence intents (guidance the AI reasons with, not a rigid tree). A playbook is **loaded as context that shapes investigation and proposal — never a script the system runs**; "prior to verify" applies at the playbook level.

**Steps are stable intent, resolved at run time.** The investigation plan encodes stable intent ("check node memory pressure on the host running the agent") and lets the AI + estate graph + connector resolve the *how* now — the stable-vs-volatile lesson, so the playbook survives estate churn.

**Judgment, not privilege** *(the key safety property)*. A playbook's remediation options reference the *existing* action catalogue — it introduces no new execution capability and can never propose anything the catalogue + approval gate wouldn't already allow. Governance (default-deny, tiered approval — ADR 0017/0021) is unchanged. Playbooks make ISE smarter about *proposing*, never more powerful about *executing*.

**Authoring — crystallised incidents.** Three sources: distilled from a resolved incident (primary/organic — Update proposes, human confirms); authored directly (seed a known class); refined by repetition (each match adds causes, reorders by frequency, updates efficacy). Maturity grows with successful reuse: resolution → candidate → confirmed → matured.

**Efficacy is the anti-rot immune system.** Each application records its outcome (did the hypothesis hold? did remediation pass validation?). Ranking uses the live success rate; a falling rate flags the playbook for review and can surface like drift ("worked 8/10, last three failed since the DB moved to Aurora"). Failed applications are as valuable as successes. Stored in Postgres, versioned with provenance so a bad edit is auditable and reversible.

## Applying the model: DataDog (worked example)

DataDog already runs the "is something wrong?" evaluation continuously, over data ISE never ingests. So:

- **Monitors → Alerts.** Only monitor state produces Alerts, keyed by `monitor/{id}/{group}` so multi-alert monitors split correctly per scope.
- **Metrics, events, logs, dashboards, service map → not issue sources.** They are **evidence, gathered on demand** during an Incident investigation to explain *why* a monitor tripped.

This avoids duplicating detection DataDog has already done and avoids surfacing signals DataDog itself doesn't consider alert-worthy. Contrast Kubernetes: no monitor layer, so ISE detects there itself → Observations.

## The Estate Knowledge Base

More than a topology map: the estate model is the shared knowledge base that makes the AI investigator competent in *this specific estate* rather than generically. It carries three registers of knowledge — all of them "what the humans and the environment know that a generic tool does not":

1. **Structural** — **entities** (services, clusters, databases, hosts, namespaces…) and typed, directed **relationships** (`runs-on`, `depends-on`, `hosted-on`, `routes-to`, `part-of`). Each entity has a canonical id, a type, and a set of integration **aliases** — how each connected system names it (`datadog:service:service-x`, `k8s:g5/team-a/deploy/service-x`). Edges carry provenance and a last-confirmed timestamp. Distinct from a `System` (a connected integration instance) — estate entities live *across and inside* Systems.
2. **Contextual** — free-form annotations that change how ISE *interprets* what it sees: behavioural caveats ("this cluster runs Karpenter, expect transient nodes on demand") and business meaning/criticality ("Kora is the company CRM, used across the business, composed of X/Y/Z"). Authored, sticky, loaded as context whenever the investigator reaches that entity.
3. **Tuning** — the severity overrides and Ignores (see *Severity…*), the structured face of the same knowledge.

All three are one idea: **capturing local human knowledge so ISE behaves correctly in this estate.** They act in two registers — *structured rules* (overrides, Ignore) applied deterministically at ingest, and *narrative context* fed into the AI's reasoning during investigation and observation. Both are wanted: structured for the known-and-repetitive, narrative for judgement.

Over time the knowledge base also accumulates **incident memory** and **playbooks** through the Incident Loop — the record of what has happened in this estate and the distilled procedures for handling it. Memory and playbooks are estate knowledge like any other; the Incident Loop is the knowledge base's read/write cycle, which is why the knowledge base is maintained by *working incidents*, not by a separate process.

**Stored in Postgres, not a graph DB.** At estate scale a few tables (entities, aliases, edges, annotations) with recursive queries suffice; a graph DB would be its own ADR and ops burden and cut against ADR 0002.

**Identity resolution** is the crux — deciding two integration names are the same entity — cheapest-first:
1. **Harvest shared keys / cross-tags automatically.** Integrations often already carry each other's coordinates (a DataDog agent in K8s tags metrics with `kube_namespace`/`kube_deployment`; K8s workloads carry `tags.datadoghq.com/service`). Where a shared key exists, resolution is free.
2. **Heuristic candidate-match** where no shared key exists — the AI proposes candidates, a human confirms; never auto-merge on a guess.
3. **Human-asserted aliases** are authoritative and sticky.

**Discovered vs authored.** Existence and placement (`runs-on`, `hosted-on`) are discovered from the integrations and refreshed on a cadence. Semantic `depends-on` edges no integration knows, criticality, context annotations, and corrections are authored and sticky. On conflict, **authored wins and the conflict is surfaced** (the "operator dismissal is sticky" pattern).

**The knowledge base is a cache, and the Obs Loop validates it.** Seeded by discovery, enriched as a byproduct of investigation (resolve-once, persist), and checked by the Obs Loop — divergence from reality ("graph says Service-X runs on g5, but it is no longer there") surfaces as a low-severity Observation. The thing that keeps a CMDB alive — noticing it has drifted — is automatic and rides the Observation machinery.

**Maintained through conversation, not forms.** The AI is the interface, over the chat surface:
- **Query** — "what does Kora depend on?", "what runs on g5?" (read-only).
- **Validate** — "is it right that Service-X depends on Database-Z?" — the AI shows its assumption, the human confirms or corrects.
- **Enrich** — "Kora is the CRM, built from these services…" — the AI turns natural language into structured graph updates.

Enrichment is a **write**, but of ISE-internal metadata, not infrastructure — so it does *not* need the tiered-action governance (ADR 0017/0021). It is audited (provenance/authored-by) and confirmed ("I'll record that Kora is the CRM and depends on X/Y/Z — confirm?"): a distinct, lightweight write class kept separate from remediation. Keeping the graph accurate is therefore a conversation, not a chore — which is the real answer to CMDB rot.

**Why it earns its place.** The estate entity id is the **join key across everything** — an Alert's affected entity, an Observation's affected infrastructure (its dedup key), and the evidence gathered during investigation all resolve to the same entity. And during investigation the knowledge base turns blind exploration into **directed, bounded traversal**: resolve the affected entity, walk typed edges to what it runs on / depends on / is depended on by (blast radius), and hand the investigator a scoped evidence plan instead of many exploratory tool round-trips. That directedness is a core part of the cost story.

## Why it's shaped this way

Detection already exists inside the sources, so ISE forwards Alerts cheaply and deterministically. ISE spends real compute only where it must — on Observations, on a slow loop — and only on confirmed signal, inside an Incident investigation a human cares about. Competence comes overwhelmingly from what ISE builds — the knowledge base, methods (playbooks) and memory — not from the raw model or integrations; and that scaffolding is simultaneously the quality play and the cost fix, because good context and method let a cheaper model do less work for a better answer. The design keeps the expensive, AI-driven work off idle timers and pointed at things that are actually happening.

## Delivery philosophy

Design the whole target before building, so the data model and capabilities are right and **nothing has to be torn up and rebuilt**. Deliver it incrementally, but only in slices that are *permanent parts of the final thing* — never throwaway scaffolding a later phase supersedes. Where feedback matters (and for a product proving its own viability, it does), deliver the **load-bearing spine first** — for the knowledge base, that is entities + aliases + resolve-during-investigation — because the remaining registers (context, tuning), drift detection and conversational enrichment all hang off that spine additively.
