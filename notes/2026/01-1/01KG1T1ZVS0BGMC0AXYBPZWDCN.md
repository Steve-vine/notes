### Requirements
- You must respond within one calendar month of receiving the request. If the request is complex or you've received multiple requests from the same person, you can extend this by a further two months, but you must inform the requester within the first month and explain why.
- Respond with all personal data you hold about them, which includes emails and Teams messages where they are mentioned by name or otherwise identifiable.
- The data should be provided in a commonly used electronic format (PDF, CSV, etc.) and be intelligible.
### Exemptions
- **Third-party data**: You may need to redact information about other identifiable individuals unless they've consented or it's reasonable to disclose without consent
- **Legal privilege**: Legally privileged communications can be withheld
- **Management planning**: Information about plans for redundancy, promotion, etc. where disclosure would prejudice the organisation
- **Confidential references**: References given (not received) in confidence
### Recent Legislative Change (Data Use and Access Act 2025)
The principle of a **"reasonable and proportionate search"** is now formally enshrined in law as of June 2025. A data controller now has the legislative backing to not search through extensive systems and data where the effort would be disproportionate to the request.

#### Request clarification and "stop the clock"
For your sales employee scenario, you could legitimately ask them to narrow the scope, for example by time period, specific projects, or particular types of communication. The one-month deadline pauses until they respond.

When evaluating whether searches would be unreasonable or disproportionate, controllers should now consider the volume of information that may need to be searched, alongside the circumstances of the request, search difficulties, and the fundamental nature of the right of access.

#### The "2000 emails" scenario
The ICO guidance specifically addresses your situation: Where a DSAR is made by a long serving employee this can involve thousands of emails and it is often a very time consuming exercise to address such a request. 

The ICO example mentions finding 2,000 emails where the employee is copied in - they recognise this is burdensome.
#### Emails where they're just copied in
For routine operational emails where they're merely a recipient, you may be able to take a proportionate approach. The ICO indicates you can assess whether these emails genuinely contain their personal data in any meaningful sense, or whether they're simply business communications.

## Practical Approach for Your Situation

1. **Acknowledge promptly** and ask for clarification on scope - what time period, what types of information are they actually seeking?
2. **Document your search methodology** - whatever approach you take, record your rationale for what systems you searched and why
3. **Apply exemptions** where relevant - client confidential information, third-party personal data, legal privilege
4. **Consider extending to 3 months** if complex, but notify them within the first month
5. **Don't search deleted/backup systems** unless you'd do so for your own purposes - The ICO will not seek to take enforcement action against an organisation that has failed to use extreme measures to recreate previously deleted personal information held in electronic form.
